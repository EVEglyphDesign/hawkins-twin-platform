## Framing

Our logistics networks will be tested in the coming years. The friction of the technology transition will show up somewhere, and every meal at the end of every day depends on a trucker getting somewhere. Anything that gives the network extra capacity, extra shifts in reserve, or extra resilience under stress reduces human suffering when times get tough. That's a worthy cause for governments to support — same principle as reinforcing the airports.

Tim's work has always cared about the people around it. This material sits alongside that — not on top of it, not directing it.

## 1. Freight electrification — where it actually is

- **Class 8 tractor electrification is slower than the press cycle implies.** Range, weight penalty, and depot-charging economics keep the addressable share modest through the late 2020s. Regional and drayage duty cycles are the near-term fit; long-haul is not.
- **Total cost of ownership is starting to break even in specific lanes** — port drayage, regional distribution, backhaul-heavy regional networks — where duty cycles fit battery range and depot charging is feasible.
- **Charging is the bottleneck, not the truck.** Depot chargers, corridor sites, and grid interconnect timelines are the gating factors.

## 2. Charging & battery infrastructure

- **Depot charging** is the near-term unlock for regional fleets. Grid upgrade timelines (12–36 months for meaningful interconnect) are the real schedule.
- **Corridor buildout** — MCS (Megawatt Charging Standard) sites along freight corridors — is what unlocks longer duty cycles. The federal corridor programs (JOET, NEVI freight extensions) are still catching up to reality.
- **Battery swap models** (Ample in the US, Janus Electric in Australia) keep resurfacing because charging time is dead time for a truck. Standardized packs across fleets would be a genuine coordination win, but nobody wants to give up proprietary control. Same lock-in pattern that shows up in software — it shows up everywhere.
- **Second-life batteries** — retired traction packs re-purposed for stationary storage at dealer sites — is a real recycling and revenue play, and sits inside the dealer's existing warranty and service relationship.

## 3. Corridor & bridge infrastructure

- The ASCE Infrastructure Report Card has not improved meaningfully. Bridges, locks, and interchange capacity are the physical constraints that no amount of software can route around.
- Reinforcing the network — extra lane capacity, redundant routing, hardened bridges — is the kind of investment that only shows its value when the next disruption hits. That's the whole point.

## 4. Dealer positioning

- The dealer sits at the intersection of the vehicle, the warranty, the parts economy, and the recycling obligation. Every one of those is a durable relationship that OEMs cannot replicate directly.
- SmartLINQ / PACCAR Solutions telemetry is proprietary but the dealer has legitimate operational visibility into it — that's the twin surface.
- Warranty actions, maintenance flows, financing events, and end-of-life recycling all flow through the dealer. A dealer-anchored digital twin is the honest place to represent them.
- Charging infrastructure, battery service, and second-life storage are natural extensions of the dealer footprint. Not a pivot — an expansion of what's already there.
